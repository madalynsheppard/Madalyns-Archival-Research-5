Pittsburgh's Most Lavish Wedding

This archive documents the wedding of Sarah Cordellia Mellon and Alan Magee Scaife. Sarah was the daughter of Jennie Taylor Mellon and Richard B. Mellon. Richard was president of the Pittsburgh Reduction Company, or ALCOA, had substantial investments in the Pittsburgh Coal Company, and established the Mellbank Corporation. Alan was the son of Hannah Mary and James Vernier Scaife, director of the Scaife Company.

The Joining of the Most Influential Families in Pittsburgh

This archive was purchased from James Cummings Booksellers of New York in April 2021. The photos were removed from their leather album to retain the leather’s integrity. The order of the photos are consistent with the album. This archive includes 4 loose photos, found in the back of the album. One of which is a group photo of Sarah Mellon’s bridal party. The album consists of photos from the Scaife’s wedding on November 26th, 1927. In total there are 26 silver gelatin printed photographs. The album gives credit to Ralph W. Johnston of Trinity Court Studios of Pittsburgh for its creation. Photos were taken of and inside the Mellon Estate, a temporary pavilion, and the East Liberty Presbyterian Church. Richard Mellon, Sarah’s father, had the temporary pavilion built on their estate, 6500 Fifth Avenue Pittsburgh, and according to a local newspaper, had cost an estimated $100,000. Photos from within the pavilion include the decorated rooms, dining settings, gift tables, flower arrangements and more.

Shaping Pittsburgh’s Future

This archive, online at the University of Pittsburgh ULS Digital Collections, is available to anyone with access to the internet. The Scaife’s influenced the city of Pittsburgh immensely. Upon the passing of Sarah’s parents, Richard and Jennie Mellon, their estate was demolished and turned into Mellon Park. The 10-11 plot of land now holds concerts, and is the home of the Ann Katherine Seaman’s memorial. The Scaife’s estate home, nicknamed Penguin Court (Boaz, 2024), was also demolished after Sarah and Richard’s death. Interestingly enough, the estate earned its name from the 10 penguins housed during its prime. Though the penguins had the ability to waddle across all 923 acres, Sarah had concrete igloos built, several of which still stand today. Before the passing of Sarah and Alan’s son Richard, who was named after Sarah’s late father, he had his parents estate and Thomas Road Farm become a preserve of Brandywine Conservatory. Sarah had also donated tens of millions to countless charities, and research studies. One of her gifts, a substantial $35,000, had been to a virus research lab at the University of Pittsburgh. This lab would go on to develop the first polio vaccine. She was also a huge donor to the Carnegie Museum of Arts and Natural Sciences. She commissioned not only an entire exhibit of miniature versions of rooms in her estate, but she donated a large sum, which led to the construction of the Sarah Mellon Scaife Gallery. This gallery had nearly doubled the exhibition space of the museum, and had cost an estimated 12.7 million dollars.The Scaife’s had two children, Cordelia and Richard Mellon Scaife. Before her death in 2005, their daughter Cordelia had spent her life fighting immigration, as she believed that the United States was “being invaded on all fronts” by immigrants who “breed like hamsters” and exhaust natural resources (Kulish, McIntire, 2019). With nearly half a billion dollars at her disposal, she would help create the modern anti-immigration movement, bankrolling the three largest restrictionist groups, the Federation for American Reform, NumbersUSA, and the Center for Immigration Studies. The Scaife’s son Richard was invested into politics, starting with backing of Sen. Barry Goldwater in the 1964 presidential election. (Diamond, 2014) He would go on to spend $1,000,000 in support of re-electing Richard Nixon, then going on to support Ronald Reagan. Later he would donate $100,000 to the Clinton Foundation, and even endorse Hilary Clinton’s 2008 presidential campaign. As the Scaife family were known Republicans, they supported associations aligned with the Republican Party’s views. They shared the Republican idea that climate change isn’t important. The Sarah Carnegie Scaife Foundation, among many others, helped to fund climate change denial groups. For many, the Scaifes helped shape the political party they associate with today, and for others it allows them to see art in all forms at the Carnegie Museum of Art and Natural Science.

Pittsburgh (and the World) Today

The Scaifes had great influence when Pittsburgh was becoming what it is today, and as it continues to develop. The Sarah Mellon Scaife Foundation has funded its fair share of projects, and depending on who is asked can be good or bad projects. In an interview Robert Brulle, a sociologist from Drexel University, spoke on the funding climate denial groups receive from some foundations; “A foundation can do pretty much whatever the hell it wants regardless of the consequences (Kennedy & Tyson, 2024). Foundations have undemocratic and plutocratic power.”. Especially in relation to political parties, it may lead to citizens realizing how much influence these foundations and philanthropists have, in regards specifically to climate change, millionaires spending obscene amounts of money to ignore issues like this. Revising policies in regard to who and what philanthropists are able to back may lead to better leaders, and more equal opportunities.
